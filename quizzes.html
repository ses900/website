Learning Quizzes

Short, focused quizzes meant to turn abstract ideas into practical intuition. Each quiz pairs a real-world scenario with a quick explanation, so you can learn by checking your reasoning.

How to use these quizzes

Step 1

Read the scenario

Visualize the situation like a lab demo, a sports clip, or a daily task. The examples are designed to anchor the math in something tangible.

Step 2

Make a quick estimate

Do a rough mental calculation before peeking at the answer. Estimation builds intuition faster than exact arithmetic alone.

Step 3

Compare and reflect

Open the explanation to see the correct reasoning and a reality check. If your answer is close, you are building the right instincts.

Quiz: Basic Physics

These questions cover forces, motion, and energy with concrete examples. Reveal each answer to review the calculation and the intuition behind it.

  1. Force and acceleration

    You push a 2 kg shopping cart and it speeds up from rest at 3 m/s². What net force are you applying?

    Show answer

    Correct: 6 N.

    Use Newton's second law: F = m × a → 2 kg × 3 m/s² = 6 N. Think of it as the steady push needed to keep the cart accelerating.

  2. Free fall speed

    A ball drops from a balcony 20 meters above the ground (ignore air resistance). About how fast is it moving just before it hits?

    Show answer

    Correct: About 20 m/s downward.

    Use v = √(2gh). With g ≈ 9.8 m/s² and h = 20 m, v ≈ √(392) ≈ 19.8 m/s. That's roughly the speed of a fast sprint or a 45 mph car.

  3. Braking acceleration

    A car slows from 15 m/s to 0 in 5 seconds at a steady rate. What is its acceleration?

    Show answer

    Correct: -3 m/s².

    Acceleration is change in velocity over time: (0 - 15) / 5 = -3 m/s². The negative sign just indicates the car is slowing down.

  4. Energy use

    A 60 W lamp runs for 2 hours. How much energy does it consume?

    Show answer

    Correct: 0.12 kWh (about 432 kJ).

    Energy is power × time. 60 W × 2 hours = 120 Wh = 0.12 kWh. In joules, 120 Wh × 3600 = 432,000 J.

  5. Momentum change

    A 0.2 kg ball moving at 10 m/s is caught and brought to rest. What impulse (change in momentum) does the catcher provide?

    Show answer

    Correct: 2 kg·m/s of impulse.

    Momentum is p = m × v. The ball goes from 0.2 × 10 = 2 kg·m/s to 0, so the impulse is 2 kg·m/s. If the catcher stops it gently, that impulse is spread out over a longer time, reducing the force.
